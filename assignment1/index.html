Design Is Alive and Thriving: A Rebuttal

Zarin Hawthorne 2024

The article's claim that "design is dead" is not only incorrect, but it also casts doubt on the diverse and dynamic range of design disciplines that exist today. Although the author raises important concerns regarding the commercialization and misunderstanding of design, calling for its death is a glaring oversimplification of a complex discipline that nevertheless has a significant impact on our society today.

First of all, design is a broad field that includes a variety of specialties, including industrial design, architecture, graphic design, user experience (UX) design, and more. It would be a mistake to deny the ongoing innovation, creativity, and problem-solving that these professions require if one were to declare design dead. Renowned design thinking expert Don Norman highlights that "Design is really an act of communication, which means having a deep understanding of the person with whom the designer is communicating." In addition to making visually beautiful things, designers are also strategists, problem solvers, and communicators who are vital in forming user interactions and experiences.

Moreover, the claim that profit-driven corporations and corporate interests have "killed" design ignores the activity of designers themselves. Although commercialization can occasionally compromise the integrity of design, many practitioners are nonetheless dedicated to moral and socially conscious design principles. According to IDEO CEO Tim Brown, "Design thinking can be a powerful force for social change. We must embrace it and use it to build a better world." Designers have the authority to oppose unethical business practices, promote sustainability, and champion user-centric design principles.

Furthermore, the digital era has created new opportunities for design innovation by enabling designers to collaborate across disciplines and reach a wider audience. The claim made by the author that "we have killed it" ignores the vibrant community of designers who are utilizing technology to produce memorable experiences. Renowned designer and engineer John Maeda says, "The intersection of technology and liberal arts is where the most exciting innovations emerge." With the help of technology, designers can now build more engaging and interactive solutions, from interactive websites to immersive virtual reality experiences.

Recognizing the close ties between design and politics, culture, and identity is also crucial. We squander the opportunity to influence cultural narratives, promote discourse, and subvert power systems when we write off design as extinct. Design is not "dead"; rather, it has the ability to arouse emotion, stimulate intellect, and motivate action. Legendary graphic designer Milton Glaser once said, "Design is the process of going from an existing condition to a preferred one." From the civil rights period to the current activism surrounding inequality and climate change, designers have always been essential to social change movements.

To sum up, to say that design is dead would be to discount its tenacity, flexibility, and continuing significance in a world that is changing all the time. The design community is undoubtedly faced with difficulties, such as commodification and commercialization, yet these difficulties do not take away from the field's energy and vigor. The enthusiasm, inventiveness, and hard work of designers worldwide keep the field of design vibrant and alive. There will always be a need for careful and creative design since there are issues to be resolved and experiences to be improved.
